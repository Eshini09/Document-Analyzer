Physical Characteristics and Atmosphere
Saturn's immense size and rapid rotation create a distinctively oblate shape, meaning it is visibly flattened at the poles and bulges at the equator. 
Composition: It is a "gas giant" made of approximately 96.3% hydrogen and 3.25% helium, with trace amounts of methane and ammonia.
Atmospheric Features: The north pole features a unique hexagonal jet stream nearly 20,000 miles wide. Winds at the equator can reach a staggering 1,100 mph (1,800 km/h).
Internal Heat: According to NASA, Saturn radiates 2.5 times more energy into space than it receives from the Sun, likely due to "helium rain" deep in its interior.

Material: They are composed of billions of individual chunks of ice and rock, ranging in size from tiny dust grains to house-sized boulders.
Structure: The main rings (A, B, and C) are relatively close together, but are separated by large gaps like the 2,920-mile-wide Cassini Division.
Thickness: Despite their vast width, the main rings are incredibly thin, typically averaging only about 30 feet (10 meters) in vertical height. 
Moons and Exploration
Saturn has a massive "family" of satellites, with over 140 moons confirmed as of recent counts. 
Titan: Saturn's largest moon is bigger than the planet Mercury and is the only moon in the solar system with a substantial atmosphere and liquid hydrocarbon lakes.
Enceladus: This small, icy moon features water-ice plumes spraying from its south pole, leading scientists to believe it may have a subsurface ocean capable of supporting life.
Key Missions: Four robotic spacecraft have visited Saturn: Pioneer 11, Voyager 1, Voyager 2, and the Cassini-Huygens mission, which orbited the planet from 2004 to 2017.
